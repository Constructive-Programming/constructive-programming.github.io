About

What is Constructive Programming all About?

Constructive Programming is a methodology for building complex software, inspired by constructive logic. It leverages the Curry–Howard–Lambek correspondence to scale software that is formally verifiable from the way it is written.

It is, in practice, about the restrictions you can impose on your code to make it easier to understand, maintain, and evolve.

There are three starting criteria for constructive programs:

  • Referential Transparency
  • Totality
  • Termination

The Libraries page lists the Scala libraries that bring constructive idioms into everyday code, and the home page introduces Fixed — a new language being designed around the same principles.

About the author

Rodolfo Hansen has been writing software professionally for more than two decades, across multiple paradigms and languages. He is currently a Global Software Quality & Craftsmanship Expert at Royal Philips in Eindhoven, where he develops static-analysis metrics around Total Program Cardinality and drives software-craftsmanship practice across Europe — covering BizDevOps, Scaled Agile, technical debt strategy, and how to keep great engineers engaged.

Before Philips he was a Software Development Manager at Otravo (Amsterdam), where he led three teams through measurable wins — shipping production ML models, refactoring a core loading pipeline from eleven hours down to fifteen minutes, and cutting defect reports by a third. Earlier he served as CTO of Bodireel (London), a health-and-fitness platform built on a stateless RESTful Scala backend with Angular and Xamarin clients, run by a tightly-knit distributed team that hit Six Sigma on incident resolution while keeping P95 latency under 20 ms.

His career as a builder of teams started in 2008 when he founded KindleIT Software Development in Santo Domingo. Over the next eight years his team shipped widely-used open-source infrastructure (the first App Engine Maven plugin), maintained an international client base, and kept turnover under 5% before he sold the company.

The thread through all of it: small, accountable, distributed teams delivering at web scale and web speed, and constantly asking what the type system could do to make wrong programs harder to write.
